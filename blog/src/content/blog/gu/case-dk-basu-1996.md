## Summary

**D.K. Basu v. State of West Bengal (1996)** — Summary

The Supreme Court of India (Justices Kuldip Singh and A.S. Anand) addressed rampant custodial violence and deaths in police lock-ups through a public interest petition. 

**Key Holdings:**

1. **Custodial violence violates Article 21:** Torture, rape, and death in police custody strike at the Rule of Law and constitute naked violations of human dignity, infringing the fundamental right to life and liberty guaranteed by Article 21.

2. **Compensation under public law:** Victims of custodial violence—and in case of death, their family members—are entitled to monetary compensation awarded by the Supreme Court or High Courts, in addition to remedies under private law (tort damages) and criminal law.

3. **11 Mandatory Requirements for Police:** The Court issued binding directives governing arrests, including: (a) police must wear visible identification, (b) memo of arrest must be attested by a witness, (c) arrestee has the right to inform a relative/friend, (d) medical examination every 48 hours during custody, and (e) inspection memo of

## Key Holdings

- Custodial violence, torture, and death in police custody constitutes a naked violation of human dignity and strikes a blow at the Rule of Law, infringing Article 21 of the Constitution.
- Compensation for established infringement of fundamental rights can be awarded under public law by the Supreme Court and High Courts, in addition to traditional remedies under private law and criminal punishment.
- The Court issued mandatory requirements that must be complied with by all police personnel and governmental agencies while arresting or detaining any person, with non-compliance rendering the official liable for departmental action and contempt of court.
- Third-degree methods of interrogation are impermissible; interrogation must be sustained and scientific, and a balanced approach is needed so that criminals do not go scot-free.
- Victims of custodial violence and family members of those who die in custody are entitled to compensation under public law, which can be adjusted against damages awarded in a civil suit.
