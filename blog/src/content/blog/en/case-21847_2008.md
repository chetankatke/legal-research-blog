## Summary
This case concerns the State of Punjab's attempt to de-notify land previously acquired for a court complex in Dasuya under Section 48 of the Land Acquisition Act, 1894, after the land was no longer needed for that purpose. The Supreme Court addressed the legal question of whether the State retains the power to de-notify land under Section 48 once possession has been taken under
## Key Holdings
- Once possession of land is taken by the State under Section 16 of the Land Acquisition Act, 1894, the land vests in the State free from all encumbrances.
- The State loses its power to de-notify acquired land under Section 48 of the Act once possession has been taken under Section 16.
- The decisive question in such cases is whether possession of the acquired land was in fact taken by the State authorities.
- Possession of the land was established on 10.7.1997 based on mutation entries and the Rapat Roznamcha of that date, showing the land was recorded for use as a court complex at Dasuya.
- The High Court rightly quashed the de-notification notification dated 23.2.2007, as the State had no power to de-notify land after taking possession under Section 16.
