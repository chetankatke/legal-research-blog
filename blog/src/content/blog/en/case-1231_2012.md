## Summary
In *Naib Subedar Naresh Chand v. Union of India*, the Supreme Court addressed whether an appeal against an Armed Forces Tribunal (AFT) order could be entertained without the appellant first seeking leave from the AFT under Section 31 of the Armed Forces Tribunal Act, 2007. Following its earlier precedent in *Union of India v. Brigadier P.S. Gill*, the Court reiterated that there is no vested right of appeal to the Supreme Court and that the appellant must first approach the AFT for leave on the ground that the dispute raises a question of law of general public importance. Since the appellant had bypassed this mandatory procedure, the Court declined to entertain the substantive grievances regarding reliefs denied by the AFT. ## Key Holdings
- There is no vested right of appeal to the Supreme Court against the final decision of the Armed Forces Tribunal (AFT).
- Every appeal to the Supreme Court from the AFT must be preceded by a plea before the AFT that the controversy raises a question of law of general public importance, and if the AFT disagrees, the appellant must apply for leave under Section 31 of the Armed Forces Tribunal Act, 2007.
- Where a component of relief prayed for is denied, the tribunal is under a mandatory duty to disclose reasons for such denial, just as it must justify the grounds on which relief is granted.
