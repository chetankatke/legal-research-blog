# Reference Court in view of the judgment of this Court in The Dollar Company vs. Collector of Madras, AIR

## Citation
- **Court:** Supreme Court of India
- **Date:** 28.9.2012
- **Judges:** _Unknown_
- **Citation:** 

## Summary
**Summary:**

This Supreme Court appeal concerned the determination of compensation for land acquired under the Land Acquisition Act, 1894, where the appellants had purchased the plot in 1972 and the land was notified for acquisition under Section 4 in 1973. The Court applied the principle laid down in *The Dollar Company vs. Collector of Madras* (AIR 1975 SC 1670), which holds that when acquisition proceedings are initiated in close proximity to a recent purchase, the actual consideration paid by the purchaser constitutes the best evidence of the land's market value. Since the appellants had failed to disclose the purchase price, the Court had issued a show cause notice as to why compensation should not be reduced
## Key Holdings
- Under the Dollar Company vs. Collector of Madras principle, when land acquisition proceedings are initiated in close proximity to a purchase, the consideration paid by the purchaser is the best evidence of the land's valuation.
- A period of 15 months between the date of purchase and the issuance of Section 4 notification under the Land Acquisition Act, 1894 constitutes close proximity warranting reliance on the purchase price for valuation.
- The purchaser-claimant bears the obligation to disclose the actual amount of consideration paid for the land; failure to do so renders the compensation determination based on extraneous considerations invalid.
- The Reference Court and High Court must determine market value under Section 28-A of the Land Acquisition Act, 1894, giving primacy to recent bona fide purchase transactions over other methods of valuation.
- The Supreme Court has the power to issue show cause notice to claimants and re-determine the entire amount of compensation when lower courts have ignored binding precedent on valuation methodology.
