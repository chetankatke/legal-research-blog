# 24776_2002

- **Court:** Supreme Court of India
- **Date:**

## Summary
The Supreme Court of India heard appeals challenging a Delhi High Court judgment that recognized consumers' fundamental right to know whether cosmetics, drugs, and food products contain ingredients of vegetarian or non-vegetarian origin. The High Court, in a PIL filed by Ozair Husain, had directed mandatory labeling on product packages—using a red symbol for non-vegetarian ingredients and a green symbol for vegetarian ingredients—drawing on Articles 19(1)(a), 21, and 25 of the Constitution to support the right to information necessary for practicing religious and personal beliefs. The High Court also ordered the Drug Controller General of India to issue a list of life-saving drugs exempted from these labeling requirements. The Supreme Court appeals, brought by the Indian Soaps & Toiletries Makers Association and the Union of India, contested these directives requiring disclosure of the origin of product ingredients.
## Key Holdings
- The High Court held that consumers have a fundamental right to know whether food products, cosmetics, and drugs available for human consumption are of non-vegetarian or vegetarian origin.
- Cosmetics must be treated at par with articles/packages of food for the purpose of disclosure of their ingredients.
- Packages of cosmetics or drugs containing non-vegetarian ingredients must carry a prescribed symbol in red colour on the principal display panel near the brand name, while those with wholly vegetarian ingredients must carry a green symbol.
- A written declaration on the package is required where a cosmetic or drug contains a mix of vegetarian and non-vegetarian ingredients, except for life-saving drugs.
- The right to disclosure of product origin is grounded in the constitutional rights to freedom of expression under Article 19(1)(a), life and personal liberty under Article 21, and freedom of religion under Article 25, as meaningful practice of beliefs requires access to relevant product information.
