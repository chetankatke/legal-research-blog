## Summary
**Summary:**

This appeal arises from an industrial dispute where the management of Sundaram Industries Ltd. dismissed 16 moulder employees for persistently refusing to follow instructions to weigh their production bags, despite earlier apologies and undertakings. The Industrial Tribunal, upheld by the Madras High Court, found the domestic inquiry fair and the charges of misconduct and disobedience proved, but set aside the dismissal as "shockingly disproportionate" to the offence. The core legal question before the Supreme Court was whether the Tribunal and High Court were justified in interfering with the punishment of dismissal despite finding the misconduct established. ## Key Holdings
- The domestic inquiry conducted by the management against the delinquent workmen was fair and proper.
- The charges of misconduct, persistent disobedience, and insubordination stood proved against the workmen who refused to follow management's instructions despite a prior apology and undertaking.
- The punishment of dismissal can be set aside by the Industrial Tribunal as shockingly disproportionate to the gravity of the offence even when the charges are otherwise proved against the delinquent workmen.
- The Industrial Tribunal has the authority to interfere with the quantum of punishment imposed by the employer when such punishment is found to be grossly disproportionate to the misconduct established.
- Refusal by workmen to comply with reasonable and lawful instructions of management, particularly when persisted in after an apology and undertaking, constitutes valid grounds for disciplinary action.
