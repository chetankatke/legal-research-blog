# 2524_2012

## Citation
- **Court:** Supreme Court of India
- **Date:** 21.12.2011
- **Judges:** _Unknown_
- **Citation:** 

## Summary
**Summary:** In this appeal, the Supreme Court considered whether the appellate court erred in admitting a winding-up petition under Section 433(e) of the Companies Act, 1956, by characterizing the company's defence as a "moonshine defence." The Court held that the defence raised by the appellant-company was genuine and substantive, noting that the company had deposited the principal amount along with interest during the pendency of the appeals, thereby demonstrating its bona fides and capacity to pay. Accordingly, the Court set aside the appellate court's order admitting the petition, while reserving liberty to the respondents to pursue their remedy through a civil suit for recovery.
## Key Holdings
- A defence raised by a company in winding-up proceedings under Section 433(e) of the Companies Act, 1956 cannot be characterized as a 'moonshine defence' merely because it disputes the claim.
- The bona fides and financial capacity of a company to pay its debts can be demonstrated by depositing the claim amount along with interest during the pendency of the appeal.
- The appellate court was not justified in admitting the company petition for winding up when the company raised a specific plea disputing the claim and showed willingness to pay.
- A defence taken to genuinely dispute liability is not the same as a sham defence raised solely to negative the creditor's claim under Section 433(e) of the Act.
- The liberty to file an appropriate civil suit for recovery of the alleged dues is reserved to the respondents, subject to the law of limitation.
