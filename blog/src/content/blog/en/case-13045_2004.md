## Summary
**Summary:**

This Supreme Court appeal arises from a Delhi High Court judgment that set aside a General Court Martial (GCM) order dismissing Ex-GNR Ajeet Singh from service and sentencing him to 7 years rigorous imprisonment for offenses including theft of grenades, INSAS rounds, and a carbine machine gun, along with unauthorized absence. The High Court held that under the Juvenile Justice (Care & Protection of Children) Act, 2000, the respondent could not be tried by GCM for offenses committed while he was a juvenile, thereby vitiating the GCM proceedings in their entirety, while permitting a fresh GCM for offenses committed after he attained 18 years of age. The core legal question is whether the JJ Act, 2000 applies to personnel subject to military law under the Army Act, 1950, and whether the GCM proceedings were valid for the period when the respondent was a minor. ## Key Holdings
- Under the Juvenile Justice (Care & Protection of Children) Act, 2000, a person who was a juvenile at the time of the offence cannot be tried by a General Court Martial for charges arising from that period.
- The General Court Martial proceedings stood vitiated in entirety to the extent they related to offences committed when the respondent was a juvenile.
- Liberty was granted to the appellant to hold a fresh General Court Martial on charges relating to offences committed by the respondent after he attained the age of 18 years.
- The findings of the General Court Martial regarding offences committed during the respondent's minority cannot be sustained and must be set aside.
- The provisions of the Juvenile Justice Act override the applicability of the Army Act, 1950 in respect of trials of juveniles for offences committed while under 18 years of age.
