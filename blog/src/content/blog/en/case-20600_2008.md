# Court in English Medium Students Parents Association vs. The State of Karnataka & Ors

## Citation
- **Court:** Supreme Court of India
- **Date:** 02.07.2008
- **Judges:** _Unknown_
- **Citation:** 

## Summary
The case concerns appeals and writ petitions challenging the Karnataka Government's language policy, which mandated the mother tongue (Kannada) as the medium of instruction in the first four standards of all recognized schools, including unaided English medium schools. The policy, originally issued via Government Order dated 19.06.1989 and reaffirmed in 1994, was challenged before the Karnataka High Court, which partly allowed the writ petitions. The Supreme Court, in a judgment authored by Justice P. Sathasivam, was called upon to determine the validity of the state's language policy and the High Court's ruling thereon, in the context of Article 350A of the Constitution and the earlier Supreme Court decision in *English Medium Students Parents Association* (1994) which had upheld the 1989 order. (The text excerpt provided ends mid-sentence, so the final holding is not fully visible, but the case centers on balancing the constitutional mandate for mother-tongue instruction with the rights of English medium schools.)
## Key Holdings
- The Supreme Court upheld the Karnataka Government Order dated 19.06.1989 specifying mother tongue as the medium of instruction at the primary school level under Article 350A of the Constitution.
- The Court held it mandatory for every child who has not opted for Kannada as the first language to take it as a second language.
- The Court ruled that the medium of instruction from 1st to 4th standard in all state-recognized schools must be either the mother tongue or Kannada with effect from Academic Year 1994-1995.
- The Supreme Court declined to interfere with the state language policy, affirming the government's authority to prescribe the medium of instruction in recognized schools.
- The Court permitted a transitional arrangement allowing existing English medium students to continue their studies while the new policy applied prospectively to new academic sessions.
