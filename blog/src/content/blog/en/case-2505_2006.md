# 2505_2006

## Citation
- **Court:** Supreme Court of India
- **Date:** 21.10.2005
- **Judges:** _Unknown_
- **Citation:** 

## Summary
**Summary:**

The Supreme Court of India heard a civil appeal filed by the Rajasthan State Road Transport Corporation challenging the High Court's affirmation of an Industrial Tribunal's award in favor of the respondent, a daily-wage bus conductor dismissed from service in 1987. The respondent had been dismissed following a departmental inquiry into allegations that during a route check, he collected fares from 20 passengers but failed to issue the corresponding number of tickets; he did not participate in the inquiry despite receiving notices. The central legal question concerned whether the dismissal was valid despite the respondent's non-appearance in the domestic enquiry and whether the principles of natural justice were adequately observed. The Court upheld the dismissal, finding that sufficient opportunity of being heard was provided to the respondent, and the inquiry officer was justified in proceeding ex parte. The Court rejected the respondent's challenge, affirming that his termination was lawful and the Civil Court's earlier finding that he had voluntarily absented himself from the enquiry proceedings was correct
## Key Holdings
- When an employee fails to appear in a departmental enquiry despite being served notices, the inquiry officer is justified in proceeding ex-parte.
- Issuing notices to the employee constitutes sufficient opportunity of being heard, discharging the employer's obligation of natural justice.
- An employer can validly dismiss an employee on a daily-wage appointment for misconduct such as collecting fares without issuing corresponding tickets.
- A finding of misconduct based on evidence led by the employer, where the employee chose not to participate in the enquiry, can sustain the dismissal order.
