## Summary
**Summary:**

In *Vivek Kalra v. State of Rajasthan*, the Supreme Court heard an appeal challenging the conviction of the appellant under Section 302 IPC and life imprisonment for the murder of his 13-year-old cousin, Ankit Kalra, based solely on circumstantial evidence. The prosecution's case rested on motive—allegedly the appellant's anger at his uncle Gurcharan Kalra's refusal to release a fixed deposit of Rs. 80,000 (meant only for the appellant's future marriage)—combined with the appellant's suspicious conduct of fleeing to Jaipur and falsely claiming to have been in an accident. ## Key Holdings
- Conviction under Section 302 IPC can be sustained on the basis of circumstantial evidence alone, even in the absence of any eyewitness to the murder.
- Motive to commit the offence, though not a sine qua non for conviction, must be established by credible evidence and cannot be inferred from a strained reading of the testimony regarding the fixed deposit.
- Mere suggestions of good behaviour or the existence of alternative suspects do not create reasonable doubt when the chain of circumstantial evidence is complete and consistent.
- An accused's attempt to create a false narrative of being assaulted or meeting with an accident shortly after the commission of the offence is a relevant circumstance indicating consciousness of guilt.
- Recovery of the dead body and the conduct of the accused in fleeing the scene and obtaining hospital treatment under a false pretext are incriminating circumstances that can establish guilt beyond reasonable doubt.
