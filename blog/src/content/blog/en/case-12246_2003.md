## Summary
This Supreme Court case concerned the central excise classification of the product "Moisturex," with the legal question being whether it qualified as a "medicament" under Chapter 30 of the Central Excise Tariff Act, 1985, attracting a 15% duty, or as a non-medicament (skin care product) attracting 70% duty. The court framed the issue as whether the product was for "care or cure" of the skin, examining the definition of "medicaments" and "patent or proprietary medicaments" under the relevant tariff notes. ## Key Holdings
- The central issue was whether the product 'Moisturex' was a medicament (taxable at 15%) or a skin care product (taxable at 70%) under the Central Excise Tariff Act, 1985.
- The product was held to be a medicament, entitling the assessee to the lower rate of duty under Heading 30.03 of the Schedule to CETA.
- The key test for classification is whether the product is intended for 'care' of the skin or for 'cure' of a condition as a therapeutic or prophylactic medicament.
- A product qualifies as a 'patent or proprietary medicament' under Note 2(ii) of Chapter 30 if it is for internal or external treatment or prevention of ailments and bears a name not specified in recognized Pharmacopoeias or is sold under a brand/trademark.
- The Tribunal's view that the product was a medicament was upheld, with the 'care or cure' determination governing the final classification.
