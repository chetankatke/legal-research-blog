# Nishant Aggarwal vs. Kailash Kumar Sharma. Nishant Aggarwal

## Citation
- **Court:** Supreme Court of India
- **Date:** 21.2.2013
- **Judges:** _Unknown_
- **Citation:** 

## Summary
This Supreme Court judgment disposes of the appeal in light of the Court's earlier decision in *Nishant Aggarwal vs. Kailash Kumar Sharma*. The key legal question was whether the court within whose jurisdiction the complainant presented the dishonoured cheque has territorial jurisdiction to entertain a petition under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act. The Court answered in the affirmative, holding that the court where the drawee/complainant presented the cheque and received it back unpaid has jurisdiction, even if the drawer's bank is located elsewhere. Following the precedent in *K. Bhaskaran*, the Court affirmed that the place where the cheque is presented for encashment and returned unpaid confers jurisdiction on that Magistrate to try the offence.
## Key Holdings
- The court within whose jurisdiction the complainant presents the dishonoured cheque has territorial jurisdiction to entertain a petition under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act.
- The court at the place where the complainant resides, maintains a bank account, and receives the bounced cheque back has jurisdiction to try the offence under Section 138.
- Territorial jurisdiction is established where the legal notice under Section 138 is sent by the complainant and received by the accused.
- The principle laid down in K. Bhaskaran governs the determination of territorial jurisdiction in cheque dishonour cases, allowing the complaint to be filed at the place of presentation, notice, or payment.
- Filing a complaint at the location where the cheque was presented for encashment and was returned unpaid is valid for conferring jurisdiction on the Magistrate.
