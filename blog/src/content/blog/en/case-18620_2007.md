## Summary
**Summary:**

In *R. Kuppusamy v. State*, the Supreme Court addressed whether the lower courts were justified in convicting the appellant of murder under Section 302 IPC based solely on an extra-judicial confession allegedly made before the Village Administrative Officer (VAO), wherein the appellant admitted to throwing his 10-month-old daughter into a well. The appellant contended that an extra-judicial confession is inherently weak evidence and must be corroborated by independent evidence to sustain a conviction, which the prosecution failed to provide. The case involves a father who allegedly killed his infant child due to suspicions about the child, with the extra-judicial confession being the principal evidence relied upon by the trial court and High Court. The Supreme Court ultimately allowed the appeal, holding that the extra-judicial confession was unreliable and uncorroborated, rendering the conviction unsustainable in law.
## Key Holdings
- An extra-judicial confession is by its very nature a weak type of evidence and must be corroborated by independent evidence to support a conviction.
- Where an extra-judicial confession is made before a person in authority like a Village Administrative Officer and reduced to writing, the conviction cannot be sustained in the absence of independent corroboration.
- The credibility of an extra-judicial confession depends on the person to whom it is made, the circumstances under which it was made, and the manner in which it was recorded.
- A conviction for murder under Section 302 IPC based solely on an extra-judicial confession without independent corroborative evidence is unsustainable in law.
- The appellate court will scrutinize extra-judicial confessions with care and require that the surrounding circumstances and corroborative evidence establish the guilt of the accused beyond reasonable doubt.
