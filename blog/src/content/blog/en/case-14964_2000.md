## Summary
In *Maharashtra Ekta Hawkers Union v. Municipal Corporation, Greater Mumbai*, the Supreme Court of India addressed the plight and rights of street vendors and hawkers in urban areas, particularly Mumbai. The case arose in the context of growing numbers of street vendors due to rural migration, lack of employment, and displacement from land acquisition, and the concomitant harassment they faced from local authorities and police. Justice G.S. Singhvi's opinion emphasized the socio-economic importance of street vendors, who serve the urban poor by providing affordable goods, and acknowledged that they had been subjected to "raw treatment" by the state apparatus, including destruction of their goods and demands for bribes. ## Key Holdings
- A street vendor or hawker is defined as a person who offers goods for sale to the public without having a permanent structure or place for his activities.
- The manifold increase in street vendors in major cities is attributable to unabated population growth, lack of employment opportunities, and forced rural-to-urban migration due to land acquisition.
- Street vendors play a vital economic role by providing cheap goods such as clothing, hosiery items, plastic wares, household items, and food to millions of urban poor and lower income groups.
- Street vendors have historically received raw treatment from the state apparatus, with officials and police regularly harassing them, extorting money, and destroying their goods and belongings.
- The court recognized that street vendors earn their livelihood with dignity and self-respect, distinguishing them from those who engage in begging, stealing, or extortion.
