## Summary
**Summary:**

This appeal before the Supreme Court arose from a suit by the plaintiff challenging alienations made by his father (defendant no. 2), including a release deed gifting 96 Kanals of land to defendant no. 1 (the father's nephew) and sale deeds for 8 Kanals. The core legal question was whether the property received by the plaintiff's father in the family partition constituted ancestral **coparcenary property**, which
## Key Holdings
- Property obtained by a coparcener in a family partition of ancestral property retains its character as coparcenary property and cannot be treated as self-acquired property.
- An alienation of ancestral coparcenary property by a father without legal necessity is null and void and not binding on his son who is a coparcener.
- A release deed executed by a coparcener in favour of a stranger purporting to transfer the entire coparcenary share does not affect the rights of the other coparceners.
- A son has locus standi to challenge alienation of ancestral property made by his father where his coparcenary rights are affected.
- Mutation entries in revenue records are merely fiscal/administrative and do not confer title or validate an otherwise void alienation.
