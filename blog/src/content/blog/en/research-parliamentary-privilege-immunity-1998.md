## Case at a Glance

### Background
The case arose from the infamous **JMM bribery scandal (1993)**. During a vote of confidence in the Lok Sabha, fourteen MPs — including Prime Minister **P.V. Narasimha Rao** and Ajit Singh, Shibu Soren, Suraj Mandal — were alleged to have received bribes from the Congress(I) to vote against the no-confidence motion. FIRs were registered in Delhi; the accused claimed immunity under **Article 105(2)** of the Constitution, arguing that MPs cannot be prosecuted for anything said or done in Parliament.

### Core Constitutional Question
Does **Article 105(2)** grant MPs immunity from criminal prosecution for bribery committed in connection with a parliamentary vote? Does such immunity extend to bribe-givers?

---

## Holdings

### Majority (Bharucha & Rajendra Babu JJ.)
| # | Holding |
|---|---------|
| 1 | MPs who took bribes were **entitled to immunity for the actual vote cast** under Art. 105(2) — they could not be prosecuted for HOW they voted |
| 2 | MPs who took bribes **could be prosecuted for the bribery itself** — Art. 105(2) does not protect the corrupt motive behind the vote |
| 3 | Bribe-givers (non-MPs) enjoyed **NO immunity** — parliamentary privilege protects only MPs |

### Minority (Agrawal & Anand JJ.)
| # | Holding |
|---|---------|
| 1 | **"In respect of"** in Art. 105(2) should be read as **"connected with"** — a wider construction |
| 2 | MPs who received bribes for voting enjoyed immunity under Art. 105(2) from bribery prosecution |

---

## Legal Principles Established

1. **Separation of vote and motive:** Art. 105(2) protects the mechanics of voting in Parliament — NOT the criminal purpose behind the vote. The vote is sacrosanct; the bribe is not.

2. **Instrument of the House doctrine:** The immunity attaches to the vote as an instrument of the House's deliberative function — not to collateral criminal conduct. The offence of bribery is severable from the protected act of voting.

3. **Functional nature of parliamentary privilege:** Privilege is a collective institutional immunity to protect Parliament's function — it cannot be appropriated as a personal shield against criminal law.

4. **India's narrower textual basis vs. UK:** India deliberately chose a narrower basis in Article 105 compared to the UK (Bill of Rights 1689), which historically extended further.

5. **Bribery and privilege are severable:** The fact that a vote was corrupted by bribery does not mean the vote itself loses privilege protection. The privilege attaches to the class of act, not to the criminality of the motive.

6. **"Arising out of" vs. "connected with":** The majority construed "in respect of" narrowly as "arising out of" — confining immunity to the vote itself. The minority dissent would have used the broader "connected with."

---

## Significance

This is the **authoritative Indian position on parliamentary privilege and criminal law**. The distinction between "immunity for the vote" vs "no immunity for the bribe" established here governs corruption prosecutions against politicians to this day. It ensures criminal law reaches corrupt politicians without Parliament becoming a sanctuary — while preventing criminal prosecutions from being used to disrupt parliamentary deliberations.

The case was decided April 17, 1998. Though PV Narasimha Rao passed away in 2004, the framework it established remains the governing law on parliamentary privilege in India.
