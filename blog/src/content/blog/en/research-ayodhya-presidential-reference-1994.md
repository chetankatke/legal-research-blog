## Case at a Glance

### Background
After the **demolition of the Babri Masjid on 6 December 1992**, President Shankar Dayal Sharma made a **Presidential Reference No. 1 of 1993 under Article 143(1)** on 7 January 1993, simultaneously with promulgating the Acquisition of Certain Area at Ayodhya Ordinance 1993 (later Act No. 33 of 1993). The Reference posed ONE question to the Supreme Court:

> *"Whether a Hindu temple or any Hindu religious structure existed prior to the construction of the Ram Janma Bhumi-Babri Masjid (including the premises of inner and outer courtyards) on the site at which the structure stood?"*

The Reference concerned 67.703 acres in Faizabad district, Uttar Pradesh (Revenue Plot Nos. 159 and 160).

### Core Issues
1. Whether the Presidential Reference was a valid/effective alternative dispute resolution
2. Constitutionality of **Section 4(3)** of the Act (which abated all pending suits including the Muslim community's adverse possession defence)
3. Whether a mosque enjoys immunity from acquisition under Articles 25-26
4. Meaning of "vest" in Section 3 — limited vs. absolute vesting
5. Legislative competence of Parliament to enact the Act

## Holdings

### Majority (Verma J. for Venkatachaliah CJI and Ray J.)
| # | Holding |
|---|---------|
| 1 | **Reference answered as "superfluous and unnecessary"** — Court declined to answer the substantive question, holding the reference was not an effective ADR mechanism |
| 2 | **Section 4(3) unconstitutional and severable** — the provision abating all pending title suits (including the Muslim community's adverse possession defence going back to 1528 A.D.) was struck down. This revived all pending suits in the Allahabad High Court |
| 3 | **A mosque is NOT an essential part of Islam** — namaz can be offered anywhere, including in the open. A mosque has no greater immunity from acquisition than temples or churches under Articles 25-26 |
| 4 | **Section 3: Limited vesting** — "vest" means a statutory receiver, not absolute owner. The Central Government holds the disputed area as a custodian pending the outcome of title suits |
| 5 | **Excess area (outside the disputed structure): Absolute vesting** — the 67.703 acres minus the disputed structure site vests absolutely in the Central Government |
| 6 | **Sections 3, 6, 7, 8 upheld** as constitutionally valid — traced to Entry 42, List III (acquisition of property) not Entry 1, List II (public order) |
| 7 | **Section 7(2) status quo** (freeze as of 7 January 1993) upheld — it preserved a lesser Hindu worship right than existed pre-demolition, and Muslim worship had de facto ceased since 1949 |
| 8 | **Solicitor General's commitment** (14 September 1994): Government would treat the Court's answer as final and binding |

### Minority (S.P. Bharucha J. for himself and A.M. Ahmadi J.)
| # | Holding |
|---|---------|
| 1 | **Sections 3, 4, and 8 entirely unconstitutional** — violating the basic structure doctrine (secularism per *Kesavananda Bharati*) |
| 2 | The majority's refusal to answer the reference would perpetuate the consequences of the mosque's demolition — itself anti-secular |
| 3 | The Act amounts to a partition of the nation on religious lines — fundamentally contrary to secularism |

## Legal Principles Established

1. **Severability doctrine applied to Section 4(3):** The invalidity of one provision (abatement of suits in S. 4(3)) does not doom the rest of the Act if the valid provisions can stand independently.

2. **Mosque not essential to Islam (Art. 25-26):** A mosque is an immovable property like any other place of worship. Islamic law (Muslim Law of India) does not grant mosques special immunity from acquisition. Namaz is not tied to a specific structure.

3. **Limited vs. absolute "vest":** The word "vest" in the Act has two meanings depending on context — limited vesting (statutory receiver) for the disputed site, absolute vesting for the adjacent excess area.

4. **Status quo as a balancing measure:** Section 7(2)'s freeze was a permissible balancing of rights — preserving a lesser Hindu worship right (since Muslim worship had ceased since 1949) without favouring either community.

5. **Entry 42 List III as legislative basis:** Parliament's power to acquire property under Entry 42, List III (Concurrent List) is sufficient notwithstanding that the primary subject (religious place of worship) might fall under Entry 1, List II (State subject) — the pith and substance doctrine applies.

6. **Presidential Reference as ADR (limits):** A Presidential Reference under Art. 143(1) cannot be used to circumvent the judicial process or short-circuit pending litigation unless both parties consent and it is truly an efficient ADR mechanism.

---

## Significance

This judgment set the **constitutional framework for the Ayodhya dispute resolution**. By striking down S. 4(3), the majority ensured all title suits were revived — ultimately leading to the **2019 Supreme Court verdict in M. Siddiq v. Mahant Suresh Das** (delivered by a 5-judge bench of the Supreme Court).

The minority's prediction that the majority's refusal to answer the reference would "perpetuate the consequences of the demolition" and be anti-secular eventually proved prescient — the very question posed in the 1994 Reference became the subject of the 2019 constitutional bench proceedings.

The finding that a mosque is not an essential part of Islam was later **reconsidered by the 2019 Supreme Court**, which took a different approach, holding the 1994 observation was not necessary to decide the case then before it.
