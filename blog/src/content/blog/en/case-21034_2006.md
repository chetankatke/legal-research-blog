## Summary
**Summary:**

In *Hiraman v. State of Maharashtra* (Criminal Appeal No. 1288 of 2008), the Supreme Court considered the legal question of the reliability and evidentiary weight of dying declarations in a murder case. The appellant challenged his conviction under Section 302 IPC, which was based solely on two dying declarations of his wife (Chandrakala), who suffered 91% burn injuries and died of cardio-respiratory failure, allegedly set on fire by the appellant after she refused his demands for gold and permission to marry her sister. The Sessions Court and the Bombay High Court (Aurangabad Bench) had both relied on these dying declarations—including the history of injuries recorded by the duty doctor—to establish the appellant's guilt, while acquitting him under Section 498-A IPC for lack of evidence. The appeal before the Supreme Court centered on whether conviction could properly rest on dying declarations alone, and the appropriate judicial approach to assessing their credibility and sufficiency as the basis for a murder conviction.
## Key Holdings
- Dying declarations, if found reliable after careful scrutiny, can form the sole basis for conviction even though the declarant is not available for cross-examination.
- Two consistent dying declarations of the deceased were held sufficient to establish that the burn injuries were caused by the appellant, sustaining his conviction under Section 302 IPC.
- The High Court rightly set aside the conviction under Section 498-A IPC for want of sufficient evidence while confirming the murder conviction based on the dying declarations.
- The defence plea that the appellant was not present near the deceased at the time of the incident was rejected as unworthy of credit.
- The conviction under Section 302 IPC carrying life imprisonment and a fine of Rs.500 with default rigorous imprisonment for three months was upheld.
