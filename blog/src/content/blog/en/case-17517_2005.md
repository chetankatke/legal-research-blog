## Summary
This Supreme Court appeal concerns whether an ex parte decree passed under Order VIII Rule 10 of the CPC (for the defendant's failure to file a written statement) can be challenged via an application under Order IX Rule 13 CPC. The Bombay High Court had held such an application to be not maintainable, relying on a division bench ruling. The Supreme Court, applying its earlier decision in *Balraj Taneja v. Sunil Madan* (AIR 1999 SC 3381), held that even when passing a decree under Order VIII Rule 10, the court must actively satisfy itself that the plaint's averments, taken as admitted, would entitle the plaintiff to judgment without requiring proof of any fact. ## Key Holdings
- Under Order VIII Rule 10 CPC, a court must not act blindly on the plaintiff's averments merely because the defendant failed to file a written statement.
- Before passing judgment under Order VIII Rule 10 CPC, the court must be satisfied that, treating the plaint's facts as admitted, a judgment could be rendered in the plaintiff's favor without requiring proof of any fact.
- The application of Order VIII Rule 10 CPC is a matter of the court's satisfaction, and judgment against a non-appearing defendant is warranted only where no facts remain to be proved due to deemed admission.
- Following the precedent in Balraj Taneja v. Sunil Madan, courts must exercise caution and independently assess the plaint's averments before invoking Order VIII Rule 10 CPC.
