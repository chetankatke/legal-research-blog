# 18924_2005

## Citation
- **Court:** Supreme Court of India
- **Date:** 
- **Judges:** _Unknown_

## Summary
This case involves two Public Interest Litigations filed before the Supreme Court of India challenging the constitutional validity of **sub-section (4) of Section 8 of the Representation of the People Act, 1951**, which protected sitting legislators from immediate disqualification upon conviction by allowing them to continue in office if they filed an appeal within 90 days. The petitioners contended that this provision was *ultra vires* the Constitution
## Key Holdings
- Section 8(4) of the Representation of the People Act, 1951 was declared ultra vires the Constitution as being inconsistent with Articles 102(1)(e) and 191(1)(e).
- A sitting Member of Parliament or State Legislature convicted of any offense and sentenced to imprisonment for a period of two years or more stands immediately and automatically disqualified from membership without a three-month grace period.
- The distinction made by Section 8(4) between sitting legislators and those seeking election, by allowing the former to avoid disqualification, was held to be arbitrary, unreasonable, and violative of the equality clause under Article 14.
- The disqualification under Section 8 of the Act applies from the date of conviction and the Legislator's seat becomes vacant on the date of such conviction, even during the pendency of an appeal.
- Parliament lacks the power to carve out an exception under Article 102(1)(e) and 191(1)(e) that permits convicted sitting legislators to continue in office, as this defeats the purpose of the constitutional disqualification provisions.
