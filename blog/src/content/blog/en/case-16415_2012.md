## Summary
**Summary:**

The Supreme Court addressed whether the High Court erred in directing a fresh recording of evidence in a Section 138 Negotiable Instruments Act proceeding by applying the *Nitinbhai Saevatilal Shah* judgment, which mandates that a successor Magistrate must re-hear a case afresh when the predecessor has partly heard it in a summary trial. The appellant Bank contended that the *Nitinbhai* principle was inapplicable because the evidence in the present case had been recorded in full, not in a summary manner. The Court, upon perusing the notes of evidence, agreed with the Bank and held that the High Court proceeded on an entirely wrong premise by treating the proceeding as a summary trial. ## Key Holdings
- In a summary trial under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act, when one Magistrate is succeeded by another, the successor Magistrate must rehear the entire case afresh and cannot continue from the stage at which the predecessor left it.
- Any order passed on the basis of evidence recorded by a predecessor Magistrate in a summary trial is not valid and must be set aside.
- The rule requiring de novo recording of evidence by a successor Magistrate applies only when the original evidence was recorded in a summary manner, not when it was recorded in full.
- Where the trial court has already recorded evidence in full rather than in summary form, the High Court cannot direct a fresh recording of evidence before another Magistrate de novo.
