## Summary
In *Mariappan v. State of Tamil Nadu*, the Supreme Court addressed whether the appellant, who killed his paternal aunt over a land dispute, was entitled to acquittal on the ground of insanity under Section 84 of the Indian Penal Code. The Court examined the evidence to determine whether the appellant was incapable of knowing the nature of his act or that it was wrong due to unsoundness of mind at the time of the offence. Rejecting the insanity plea, the Court held that the prosecution had successfully established the appellant's guilt, and the concurrent findings of the trial court and High Court warranted no interference. The appeal was accordingly dismissed, and the conviction and sentence were confirmed.
## Key Holdings
- The burden of proving the defense of insanity under Section 84 IPC lies on the accused, who must establish it on a preponderance of probabilities.
- Mere abnormality of mind, strange behavior, or prior mental incapacity short of total deprivation of reason does not amount to legal insanity under Section 84 IPC.
- To succeed under Section 84 IPC, the accused must show that at the time of the offense he was incapable of knowing the nature of the act or that it was wrong or contrary to law.
- The opinion of an expert medical witness is relevant but not conclusive, and the court must independently assess whether the statutory requirements of Section 84 are satisfied.
- Where the accused's conduct immediately after the crime shows awareness, planning, and attempt to escape, the plea of insanity is rendered highly improbable.
