# 24177_2011

## Summary
**Summary:**

This Supreme Court appeal arose from the Himachal Pradesh High Court's reversal of the trial court's acquittal, wherein the appellant was convicted of raping an 11-year-old girl under Section 376 IPC and sentenced to five years' rigorous imprisonment. The High Court found the prosecution's case credible based on the prosecutrix's testimony, corroborated by her companion (PW-2), the companion's mother (PW-3) who responded to the alarm, and the medical evidence of Dr. Suresh Bansal, while acquitting the co-accused Dinesh Kumar. The central legal question before the Supreme Court concerned whether the High Court was justified in overturning the trial court's acquittal and convicting the appellant on the basis of the evidence on record. The case thus engages with the well-settled principle that appellate courts must exercise caution in disturbing a trial court's acquittal, requiring compelling reasons to do so.
## Key Holdings
- The High Court was justified in reversing the trial court's acquittal and convicting the appellant for rape under Section 376 IPC where the prosecution story was found reliable and inspired confidence.
- In cases of sexual assault on minor victims, the deposition of the prosecutrix carries significant weight and can form the basis of conviction when found credible.
- Corroboration of the prosecutrix's testimony by eyewitnesses, such as the companion who escaped assault and the rescuer mother, strengthens the prosecution case against the accused.
- Medical evidence from the examining doctor establishing commission of rape upon the victim is a crucial factor supporting the conviction of the accused.
- Where the trial court erroneously acquits the accused despite reliable evidence, the appellate court has the power to re-evaluate the evidence and set aside the acquittal.
