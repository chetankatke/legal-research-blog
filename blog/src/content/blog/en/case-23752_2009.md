## Summary
This landmark Supreme Court of India case, *Novartis AG v. Union of India*, addressed the interpretation of Section 3(d) of the Patents Act, 1970, and whether the beta crystalline form of Imatinib Mesylate (marketed as Glivec) qualified as a patentable invention. The central legal question was whether the new form of the known drug satisfied the requirements of being a "new product" with enhanced therapeutic efficacy over the existing compound, as required under Section 3(d). The Court denied Novartis's patent application, holding that the beta crystalline form was merely a new form of a known substance without demonstrating significantly enhanced therapeutic efficacy, and therefore did not constitute an "invention" under the Act. The judgment sought to balance the promotion of pharmaceutical innovation with preventing evergreening of patents, reinforcing India's commitment to keeping essential medicines affordable, particularly for developing nations dependent on generic drugs.

What is the true import of section 3(d) of the Patents Act, 1970?  How does it 
interplay with clauses (j) and (ja) of section 2(1)? Does the product for which the appellant 
claims patent qualify as a “new product” which comes by through an invention that has a 
feature that involves technical advance over the existing knowledge and that makes the 
invention “not obvious” to a person skilled in the art?  In case the appellant’s product 
satisfies the tests and thus qualifies as “invention” within the meaning of clauses (j) and 
(ja) of section 2(1), can its patentability still be questioned and denied on the ground that 
section 3(d) puts it out of the category of “invention”? On the answer to these questions 
depends whether the appellant is entitled to get the patent for the beta crystalline form of a 
chemical compound called Imatinib Mesylate which is a therapeutic drug for chronic 
myeloid leukemia and certain kinds of tumours and is marketed under the names “Glivec” 
or “Gleevec”.
